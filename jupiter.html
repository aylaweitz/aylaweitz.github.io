Modeling the Orbits of Jupiter's Galilean Satellites

My final project for Astro 120 — Optical and Infrared Astronomy Lab. We observed Jupiter on the Leuschner telescope on November 4, 2021 from 6:46 to 9:56pm PST.

Frames taken throughout our observation. Video made by group member Ben Terao.

To obtain Jupiter's coordinates in our CCD we implemented an edge detection filter. To find the positions of the moons, we used DAOStarFinder to find their centroid coordinates. We subtracted the moons' coordinates by Jupiter's coordinates to obtain the moon's coordinates relative to Jupiter.

After obtaining the relative moon positions over the course of our observation, we were able to fit the following parametric equations with MCMC:

  • xn = an cos(ωn (t + dn)) • cos (cn) − an cos (in) • sin(ωn (t + dn)) • sin (cn)
  • yn = an cos (ωn (t + dn)) • sin (cn) + an cos (in) • sin (ωn (t + dn)) • cos (cn)

where an is length of semimajor axis (radius of approximately circular orbit) in km, in is angle of inclination of orbit towards the viewer, cn is angle of CCW rotation of semimajor axis from CCD horizontal, dn is a CCW time displacement from (1,0) of an untransformed orbit, ωn is the orbital frequency (radians/min), t is the time in minutes after the first frame.
